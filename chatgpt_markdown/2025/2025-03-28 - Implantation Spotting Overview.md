Implantation spotting is light bleeding that can occur when a fertilized egg attaches to the lining of the uterus. It typically happens around 6–12 days after ovulation and fertilization, which is usually a few days before a person expects their period.  

### **Key Features of Implantation Spotting:**

### **Implantation Bleeding vs. Period:**  
| Feature | Implantation Spotting | Period |  
|---------|----------------------|--------|

### **When to Take a Pregnancy Test?**  
- For the most accurate results, take a home pregnancy test **a few days after a missed period** (or at least 12–14 days after ovulation).

If the bleeding is heavy, prolonged, or accompanied by severe pain, it’s best to consult a doctor to rule out other causes.

If she just took the morning-after pill (emergency contraception), implantation spotting is unlikely because the pill works primarily by delaying ovulation or preventing fertilization. However, some spotting or irregular bleeding can occur as a side effect of the pill.  

### **What to Expect After Taking the Morning-After Pill:**  
1. **Spotting or Bleeding** – Light bleeding or spotting can happen within a few days after taking the pill and may last a few days.  
2. **Changes in the Next Period** – The next period may come earlier or later than expected and may be lighter or heavier than usual.  
3. **Other Side Effects** – Nausea, fatigue, breast tenderness, headache, or mild cramping may occur.  

### **When to Take a Pregnancy Test?**  
- If her period is more than **a week late**, take a pregnancy test to confirm if the pill was effective.  
- A test taken **too early (less than 10–14 days after unprotected sex)** may not be accurate.  

If she experiences severe abdominal pain, heavy prolonged bleeding, or other unusual symptoms, it’s best to see a doctor.
