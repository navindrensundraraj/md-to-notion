DOT 3 and DOT 4 are both glycol-based brake fluids, but they have key differences that affect performance and compatibility:

### **1. Boiling Points (Performance)**
- **DOT 3**  
  - Dry Boiling Point: **205°C (401°F)**  
  - Wet Boiling Point: **140°C (284°F)**  
- **DOT 4**  
  - Dry Boiling Point: **230°C (446°F)**  
  - Wet Boiling Point: **155°C (311°F)**  
- DOT 4 has a higher boiling point, making it more resistant to brake fade under high heat, which is beneficial for aggressive driving or high-performance applications.

### **2. Water Absorption (Hygroscopic Nature)**
- Both DOT 3 and DOT 4 absorb moisture over time, but DOT 4 absorbs water at a **slower rate**.  
- However, DOT 4 is more sensitive to water contamination and requires **more frequent changes** to maintain its higher boiling point.

### **3. Compatibility**
- DOT 4 can replace DOT 3 in most vehicles, but **not vice versa** in systems designed for DOT 4.  
- Both are compatible with rubber seals in brake systems but should **not be mixed** with DOT 5 (silicone-based).

### **4. Price and Availability**
- DOT 3 is generally cheaper and more common in older vehicles.  
- DOT 4 is slightly more expensive but provides better performance, especially in modern or high-performance vehicles.

### **Which One Should You Use?**
- **For daily driving & older cars** → DOT 3 is sufficient.  
- **For spirited driving, high temperatures, or newer cars** → DOT 4 is the better choice.  
- Always check your vehicle’s manual to confirm the recommended brake fluid type.
