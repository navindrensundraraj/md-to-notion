# Wafer Yield Calculation

Explain why 17 wafers for every 25mm ingot length obtained

In semiconductor manufacturing, silicon wafers are sliced from a silicon ingot, which is a cylindrical piece of single-crystal silicon. The number of wafers you can obtain from a certain length of an ingot depends on the thickness of each wafer, the thickness of the saw cut (kerf loss), and the total length of the ingot.

### Here's how you might arrive at 17 wafers from a 25mm ingot length:

1. **Wafer Thickness**: The standard thickness of a silicon wafer can vary, but let's assume the wafers are 1 mm thick.

2. **Kerf Loss**: The kerf loss is the material lost due to the thickness of the saw blade used to slice the ingot. Let's assume the kerf loss is 0.2 mm per wafer.

3. **Total Material per Wafer**: The total material required for each wafer includes the wafer thickness plus the kerf loss. So, each wafer would require 1.2 mm of the ingot.

4. **Total Number of Wafers**: To calculate the number of wafers from a 25 mm ingot:
   \[
   \text{Number of wafers} = \frac{\text{Ingot Length}}{\text{Wafer Thickness + Kerf Loss}} = \frac{25 \text{ mm}}{1.2 \text{ mm}} \approx 20.83
   \]

   Since you can't have a fraction of a wafer, you'd get 20 full wafers.

If 17 wafers are obtained, it suggests that either:
- The wafer thickness or kerf loss is greater than assumed (e.g., thicker wafers or more material lost per cut).
- The ingot length used in the calculations or the assumptions made (like tolerances or additional processing considerations) might differ.

Without specific details on the exact thickness and kerf loss, 17 wafers from a 25mm ingot likely reflects particular choices made in the process, such as larger wafer thickness or greater kerf loss.
