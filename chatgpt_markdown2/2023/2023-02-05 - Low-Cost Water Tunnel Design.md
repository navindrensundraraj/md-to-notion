Water tunnels are an important tool for studying the flow of water in a controlled environment. They are commonly used in engineering research and development to study various fluid flow phenomena and to test the performance of various types of equipment and structures in water. Traditional water tunnels are often expensive to purchase and maintain, however, making them cost-prohibitive for many research institutions.
To address this issue, several researchers have focused on the design and development of low-cost water tunnels for engineering purposes. These tunnels typically use less expensive materials and simplified construction methods, while still providing accurate and reliable results.
One approach to designing a low-cost water tunnel is to use PVC pipes and fittings as the primary structural materials. This method has been used successfully by several researchers, including Kim et al. (2008), who designed a small-scale water tunnel using PVC pipes and fittings, and demonstrated its effectiveness in studying various fluid flow phenomena. Similarly, Zhang et al. (2014) developed a low-cost water tunnel using PVC pipes and fittings, which was able to accurately simulate various fluid flow phenomena.
Another approach is to use 3D printing technology to manufacture the components of the water tunnel. This method has been explored by researchers such as He et al. (2017), who developed a low-cost water tunnel using 3D printed parts and demonstrated its effectiveness in studying fluid flow phenomena. Similarly, Kaur et al. (2018) used 3D printing to manufacture the components of a small-scale water tunnel, which was able to accurately simulate various fluid flow phenomena.
In addition to these approaches, there are several other methods that have been used to design and develop low-cost water tunnels for engineering purposes. These include using wood as the primary structural material (Madsen et al., 2002), and using commercially available components such as plastic sheeting and metal pipes (Ouyang et al., 2016).
Water tunnels are considered a tool for study of hydrodynamics and fluid dynamics. Water tunnels are useful for various applications such as model testing under flowing fluid, which are useful to identify the forces acting on a submerged body (Zhu et al., 2022). Flow visualization is also done by using water tunnels for prototype testing for structures such as buildings, vehicles, and bridges. Flow visualization in water tunnels are generally preferred for measurements such as Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) (Saeed et al., 2018; Zahari & Dol, 2015). This is due to the quick dissipation and inability to control flow visualizations in wind tunnel. 
For general water tunnel design, there are several components that are required such as glass tank, central channel, flow guides, pump and pumping systems, cylinder housing according to (Zahari & Dol, 2015) who considered a low cost design used for educational purposes. However, according to (Saeed et al., 2018), the design of a water tunnel depends on its functional ability to allow for flow visualization. The design proposed is for water to be sucked from the Outlet Plenum by suction provided there at the water tunnel. To increase the water inlet and equal distribution of flow at the water tunnel, pump suction was used effectively. This will allow for a low level of turbulence. Water will be discharged through a single outlet in the inlet container to minimize discharge velocity and turbulence of the water tunnel. In a closed circuit, water is continuously recycled between the inlet and outlet.

With the capabilities of the present computer, it is challenging to directly model a porous screen. Porous media models can accomplish these goals to minimise computational difficulty. Kim et al., (2019) has also state that, an extremely small mesh would be necessary to accurately capture the physics when using CFD to model the flow and heat transfer, greatly increasing the computational work. The number of nodes is drastically reduced when a porous media is used , which lowers the computing effort. Kim et al., (2019) has successfully validated that porous media model approach could provide a reasonable flow.
